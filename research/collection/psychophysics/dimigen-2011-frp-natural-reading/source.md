# Coregistration of eye movements and EEG in natural reading: analyses and review

**Authors:** Olaf Dimigen, Werner Sommer, Annette Hohlfeld, Arthur M. Jacobs, Reinhold Kliegl
**Journal:** Journal of Experimental Psychology: General 140(4):552-572 (2011)
**DOI:** 10.1037/a0023885
**PMID:** 21744985

> Note: APA paywall. Abstract reproduced from PubMed.

## Abstract

Brain-electric correlates of reading have traditionally been studied with word-by-word presentation, a condition that eliminates important aspects of the normal reading process and precludes direct comparisons between neural activity and oculomotor behavior. In the present study, we investigated effects of word predictability on eye movements (EM) and fixation-related brain potentials (FRPs) during natural sentence reading. Electroencephalogram (EEG) and EM (via video-based eye tracking) were recorded simultaneously while subjects read heterogeneous German sentences, moving their eyes freely over the text. FRPs were time-locked to first-pass reading fixations and analyzed according to the cloze probability of the currently fixated word. We replicated robust effects of word predictability on EMs and the N400 component in FRPs. The data were then used to model the relation among fixation duration, gaze duration, and N400 amplitude, and to trace the time course of EEG effects relative to effects in EM behavior. In an extended Methodological Discussion section, we review 4 technical and data-analytical problems that need to be addressed when FRPs are analyzed: (1) the synchronization of EEG and eye-tracker data, (2) the removal of large eye-movement-related artifacts from the EEG, (3) the temporal overlap between potentials from successive fixations, and (4) the influence of low-level oculomotor and stimulus parameters on the FRP waveforms.

## Coverage

- Simultaneous EEG and video-based eye tracking during free reading.
- Fixation-related potentials (FRPs) time-locked to first-pass fixations.
- N400 effects of word predictability replicated under free-viewing conditions.
- Methodological discussion of synchronization, artifact removal, temporal overlap, and low-level confounds.

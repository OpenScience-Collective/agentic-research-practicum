# Natural vision reveals regional specialization to local motion and to contrast-invariant, global flow in the human brain

**Authors:** Andreas Bartels, Semir Zeki, Nikos K. Logothetis
**Journal:** Cerebral Cortex 18(3):705-717 (2008). Epub 2007 Jul 5.
**DOI:** 10.1093/cercor/bhm107
**PMID:** 17615246

> Note: Oxford Academic / Cerebral Cortex paywall. Abstract reproduced from PubMed; key methodology summarized in `card.md`.

## Abstract

Visual changes in feature movies, like in real-life, can be partitioned into global flow due to self/camera motion, local/differential flow due to object motion, and residuals, for example, due to illumination changes. We correlated these measures with brain responses of human volunteers viewing movies in an fMRI scanner. Early visual areas responded only to residual changes, thus lacking responses to equally large motion-induced changes, consistent with predictive coding. Motion activated V5+ (MT+), V3A, medial posterior parietal cortex (mPPC) and, weakly, lateral occipital cortex (LOC). V5+ responded to local/differential motion and depended on visual contrast, whereas mPPC responded to global flow spanning the whole visual field and was contrast independent. mPPC thus codes for flow compatible with unbiased heading estimation in natural scenes and for the comparison of visual flow with nonretinal, multimodal motion cues in it or downstream. mPPC was functionally segregated from area V6, which lies medially to it.

## Coverage

- Decomposition of feature-movie input into global flow (camera/self-motion), local flow (object motion), and residual (illumination, cuts).
- Voxel-wise correlation of each component with BOLD response in MT+, V3A, mPPC, V6, LOC.
- Predictive-coding-consistent finding: early visual cortex tracks only residual mismatches, not motion-explained variance.
- Contrast independence as a signature of global-flow integration in mPPC.

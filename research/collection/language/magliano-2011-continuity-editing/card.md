# The Impact of Continuity Editing in Narrative Film on Event Segmentation

## TL;DR

Continuity editing manipulations in narrative film modulate viewer event-segmentation behavior, supporting the claim that shot boundaries function as perceptual event markers regardless of dialogue.

## Summary

Magliano and Zacks ran behavioral experiments in which film clips were edited with either standard continuity (matched-action, same-spatial-frame cuts) or violations of continuity. Viewers identified more event boundaries in clips with discontinuous edits, and recall was modulated accordingly. The findings extend Zacks Event Segmentation Theory into the film domain and argue that cuts themselves are processed as candidate event boundaries by the perceiver, modulated by whether they preserve narrative continuity. The work bridges cognitive psychology of film and the neuroscience of event segmentation.

## Relevance to the review

High relevance because ThePresent is parsed exactly along shot boundaries. This paper provides the cognitive-psychology basis for treating shot onsets as candidate event-boundary markers and predicts that the brain should respond to a shot transition with an event-boundary signature. Fully transfers to silent animation: continuity editing is a visual phenomenon and the experiments did not require speech.

## Notable details

- Continuity-edited vs. non-continuity-edited clips; behavioral segmentation task.
- Cuts increase boundary identification; non-continuity cuts more so.
- Connects Zacks Event Segmentation Theory to applied film editing.
- No speech manipulation; works on visual continuity alone.

## Open questions / limitations

- Behavioral only, no neuroimaging.
- Adult viewers; no developmental data.
- Stimuli are short Hollywood-style live-action clips, not animation.

# Autism, Asperger syndrome and brain mechanisms for the attribution of mental states to animated shapes

## TL;DR

Adults with autism or Asperger syndrome show reduced activation in the mentalising network during silent geometric-shape animations that imply mental states, supporting a domain-specific account of social-cognitive atypicality independent of language.

## Summary

Castelli, Frith, Happe, and Frith ran the same silent-animation paradigm as Castelli 2000 in adults with high-functioning autism / Asperger syndrome. Compared with neurotypical controls, mentalising clips elicited reduced activation in mPFC, TPJ, and superior temporal sulcus, despite intact behavioural identification of motion. The result was strong early evidence for a social-brain dysfunction in autism that does not require language. Cited extensively in autism-fMRI literature.

## Relevance to the review

Medium-high relevance; survives silent-stimulus boundary. For HBN, which includes a substantial fraction of children with autism-related diagnoses, this suggests that mentalising-related responses to social shots in ThePresent could differ between subgroups. Cited as background for individual-difference / clinical analyses, not as primary contrast.

## Notable details

- Same silent-animation paradigm as Castelli 2000.
- 10 adults with high-functioning autism vs. neurotypical controls.
- Reduced mPFC, TPJ, pSTS engagement during mentalising clips.
- No language demand; motion-implied mental states only.

## Open questions / limitations

- Small clinical sample, adults only.
- Geometric-shape stimuli; ThePresent is rich Pixar animation.
- fMRI; no EEG translation.

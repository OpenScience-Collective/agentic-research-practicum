# Visual speech speeds up the neural processing of auditory speech

## TL;DR

Visible mouth movements that precede heard syllables both speed and reduce the auditory N1/P2 ERP, providing physiological evidence that vision pre-activates auditory cortex during speech perception.

## Summary

van Wassenhove, Grant, and Poeppel measured ERPs while subjects perceived auditory, visual, and audiovisual presentations of /pa/, /ka/, /ta/. In the audiovisual condition the auditory N1 and P2 latencies were earlier and amplitudes smaller compared with audio-alone, scaled with the predictive power of the visible mouth movement. The result was a foundational demonstration of "analysis-by-synthesis": vision generates a predictive forward model that suppresses redundant auditory cortex activity. It anchored multisensory speech research for the following decade.

## Relevance to the review

Boundary-case reference: a paradigmatic audiovisual-integration ERP study. For ThePresent the relevance is medium-low because the stimulus contains no speech; visible mouths in the animation may exist, but there is no auditory speech to be predicted from them. Useful as comparator for what audiovisual integration looks like in EEG when the dominant cross-modal pairing is speech, vs. what is left when speech is removed (likely scene/object onset cues only).

## Notable details

- Audiovisual condition shortened auditory N1 latency by ~10-20 ms and reduced amplitude.
- Effect graded by predictive power of mouth movement (/pa/ > /ka/ > /ta/ in visual predictability).
- 26 participants, syllable-level stimuli.
- Citation node for the analysis-by-synthesis account of multisensory speech.

## Open questions / limitations

- Syllable-level stimuli, not naturalistic speech.
- No silent animation analogue; the predictive cross-modal effect depends on the auditory channel.
- Adult sample only.

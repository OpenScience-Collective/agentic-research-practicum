# Evidence of a predictive coding hierarchy in the human brain listening to speech

## TL;DR

Hierarchical predictive coding emerges naturally when comparing fMRI responses during story listening to GPT-2 with progressively longer prediction horizons; cortical regions form a gradient from short-term phonological prediction to long-term narrative prediction.

## Summary

Caucheteux, Gramfort, and King scanned 304 adults listening to short stories. They augmented GPT-2 with explicit prediction-horizon variations and regressed each variant against fMRI. Short-horizon predictions best explained early auditory cortex; longer horizons best explained inferior frontal and parietal cortex. They argued for a temporal-hierarchy of prediction that mirrors Lerner 2011 TRWs, but anchored to a specific computational model. Public code released.

## Relevance to the review

Low relevance for ThePresent. Like Schrimpf 2021, the hierarchy is anchored to spoken-word prediction and does not survive removal of speech. Cited as comparator to make explicit that the "predictive coding hierarchy in narrative listening" framework requires speech.

## Notable details

- 304 adults, story-listening fMRI.
- GPT-2 augmented with prediction-horizon manipulation.
- Cortical hierarchy of prediction horizons mapped.
- CC-BY release.

## Open questions / limitations

- Speech-only stimuli.
- GPT-2 surface-frequency confound only partially controlled.
- No silent-stimulus equivalent.

# Crossmodal binding through neural coherence: implications for multisensory processing

## TL;DR

Cross-modal binding of audio and video events is supported by transient gamma-band synchronization and by phase coherence between modality-specific cortical networks.

## Summary

Senkowski, Schneider, Foxe, and Engel review electrophysiological evidence that multisensory integration involves transient phase coupling, especially in the gamma band, between auditory and visual cortex. They contrast feed-forward models (where multisensory binding is computed in higher-order convergence zones) with oscillation-based binding (where temporal coherence across modality-specific regions is the binding mechanism). They argue that EEG/MEG data favour the second view and that low-frequency phase alignment plus gamma-band power increases jointly mark audiovisual integration. Key reference for any multisensory time-frequency study.

## Relevance to the review

Methodological reference for time-frequency analyses on movie EEG. Relevance medium for ThePresent because the canonical audiovisual demonstrations rely on synchronous AV stimuli; in a silent animation, only the visual stream and any music remain. Their framework still applies if "modalities" are broadened to scene-level visual features and global luminance/motion energy, but the cited evidence base is audio-visual.

## Notable details

- Gamma-band power increases at AV onset relative to unimodal.
- Low-frequency phase alignment between auditory and visual cortex during integration.
- Frames audiovisual integration as a coupling phenomenon, not a hierarchical convergence.
- Cited heavily in subsequent oscillatory-binding work.

## Open questions / limitations

- Review only; no original dataset.
- Most demonstrations are short-duration AV beep-flash paradigms.
- Silent-stimulus boundary not addressed.

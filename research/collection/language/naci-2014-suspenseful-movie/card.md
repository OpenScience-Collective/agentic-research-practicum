# A common neural code for similar conscious experiences in different individuals

## TL;DR

During passive viewing of a suspenseful Hitchcock movie excerpt, executive and frontoparietal cortices show highly synchronized activity across viewers that tracks the suspense profile, providing a covert assay for conscious experience.

## Summary

Naci, Cusack, Anello, and Owen scanned healthy adults and patients with disorders of consciousness watching an 8-minute excerpt of Alfred Hitchcock's "Bang! You're Dead". Inter-subject correlation in frontoparietal executive cortex tracked an independently rated suspense time-course, and a behaviorally non-responsive patient showed similar frontoparietal synchronization. The paper established naturalistic-movie ISC as a covert probe of higher-order conscious processing. Although the excerpt is audiovisual, the analysis is predominantly visually driven.

## Relevance to the review

High relevance because it is the canonical reference for "movie watching as a probe of higher-order cortex" and because the Hitchcock excerpt is famously language-light, with most narrative driven by visual suspense and editing. For ThePresent, supports the methodological premise that a 3.5-minute silent animation can drive reproducible high-order cortical responses. Boundary case: stimulus is technically audiovisual, but most of the demonstrated effect is driven by the visual suspense profile. The result largely transfers.

## Notable details

- 8-minute Hitchcock excerpt; mostly visual narrative.
- Frontoparietal ISC tracks rated suspense.
- Used as a covert consciousness probe in patients.
- Heavily cited in clinical fMRI / consciousness research.

## Open questions / limitations

- Audiovisual stimulus; dialogue is sparse but present.
- Healthy adult sample plus single patient.
- BOLD timescale only; no ms-resolution.

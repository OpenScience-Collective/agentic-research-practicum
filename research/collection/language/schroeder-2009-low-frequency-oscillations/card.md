# Low-frequency neuronal oscillations as instruments of sensory selection

## TL;DR

Low-frequency neuronal oscillations align their phase to attended events, creating periodic windows of high gain that gate sensory processing across modalities.

## Summary

Schroeder and Lakatos review evidence that delta- and theta-band oscillations in sensory cortex entrain to the timing of behaviorally relevant events (visual, auditory, or audiovisual). High-excitability phases coincide with predicted event onsets and amplify stimulus-evoked responses. Low-excitability phases attenuate distractor processing. The framework unifies results from monkey ECoG, human EEG, and behavioral entrainment studies. It became the dominant theoretical framework for cross-modal attentional gating.

## Relevance to the review

Strong methodological relevance for any time-frequency analysis of movie EEG. In ThePresent, shot onsets are themselves the rhythmic events; if delta/theta phase entrains to shot boundaries, the 0-500 ms post-onset ERSP will be sensitive to that entrainment. Boundary survival: yes, the framework is modality-general and does not require speech; rhythmic visual events are sufficient.

## Notable details

- Phase resetting at attended event onsets across modalities.
- Delta-band entrainment in primary visual cortex is well documented.
- Cross-modal gain modulation predicted from oscillatory phase.
- Foundational for entrainment-based predictive-timing accounts.

## Open questions / limitations

- Review; no original data.
- Most cited evidence is from primate ECoG, not pediatric EEG.
- Does not address shot-level event statistics directly.

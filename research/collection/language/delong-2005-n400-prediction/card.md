# Probabilistic word pre-activation during language comprehension inferred from electrical brain activity

## TL;DR

Article ("a"/"an") that violates the predictability of an upcoming noun produces a graded N400, demonstrating that readers pre-activate phonological form of likely words during sentence comprehension.

## Summary

DeLong, Urbach, and Kutas presented sentences in which a highly predictable noun was preceded by either the expected indefinite article or its alternate form ("a kite" vs "an airplane"). N400 amplitude on the article scaled inversely with the cloze probability of the noun it implied. This was strong evidence for graded probabilistic pre-activation of upcoming linguistic content, foundational for the prediction-error account of language comprehension. Subsequent replications (Nieuwland et al. 2018) have qualified the original effect size but the qualitative phenomenon stands.

## Relevance to the review

Reference paper for prediction-based N400 effects. Marked low for ThePresent because the effect depends on phonological article-form pre-activation in written sentences, with no analogue in a silent animation. Useful only as boundary citation: an entire class of "prediction" effects in the EEG literature requires phoneme- or word-level alignment that is unavailable here.

## Notable details

- Graded N400 amplitude on article position before predictable noun.
- Effect depends on probabilistic pre-activation, not lexical-semantic mismatch on the noun itself.
- Subsequent multi-lab replication (Nieuwland 2018) found a smaller but reliable effect.

## Open questions / limitations

- Reading-only paradigm; English-specific article distinction.
- Multi-lab replication shrunk the effect; ecological validity outside structured sentences is debated.
- No transfer to silent animation: no articles, no lexical pre-activation.

# Shared memories reveal shared structure in neural activity across individuals

## TL;DR

During free recall of an audiovisual movie, default-mode regions reactivate the same event-specific spatial patterns evoked at encoding, even though no perceptual input is present, indicating shared neural structure for narrative events across encoding and retrieval.

## Summary

Chen, Leong, Honey, Yong, Norman, and Hasson scanned participants while they watched a 50-minute episode of "Sherlock" and again during free verbal recall. They identified ~50 event boundaries and showed that within-event multivoxel patterns in DMN regions correlated across viewing and recall, on an event-by-event basis. The result extended event segmentation theory to the neural level: the brain encodes movies as discrete events with reproducible high-dimensional spatial patterns. Foundational for the Hippocampal-DMN event-structure literature.

## Relevance to the review

Medium-high relevance for the conceptual framing of "shot as event boundary". In ThePresent each shot is a candidate event; this paper supports the prediction that shot transitions should produce event-boundary signatures. Boundary survival: the original stimulus is audiovisual with dialogue. The event-boundary phenomenon is about scene changes and would be expected to transfer to a silent animated short, but the paper does not isolate the contribution of dialogue from the contribution of visual cuts.

## Notable details

- 50-minute Sherlock episode; recall scanned separately.
- Event-specific multivoxel patterns reactivate at recall.
- Effect strongest in posterior medial cortex, angular gyrus, mPFC.
- Anchors the "DMN as event-structure code" claim.

## Open questions / limitations

- Audiovisual stimulus with dense dialogue.
- Adult sample.
- Slow BOLD timescale; cannot speak to ms-scale shot-onset dynamics.

# Activation of human primary motor cortex during action observation: a neuromagnetic study

## TL;DR
Magnetoencephalography (MEG) of the rolandic 15-25 Hz rebound shows that human primary motor cortex (M1) is selectively activated during passive observation of hand actions, providing the first non-invasive electrophysiological evidence for an observation-execution matching system in humans.

## Summary
Eight participants underwent median-nerve stimulation while at rest, manipulating an object, or watching the experimenter perform the same manipulation. The post-stimulus rebound of rolandic ~20 Hz oscillatory activity was strongly suppressed bilaterally during execution and significantly diminished during observation (31-46% of the execution-related suppression). Source modeling localized the rebound to precentral motor cortex. Control conditions (LED color changes; random dot motion) confirmed the suppression was specific to action viewing rather than generic attention or motion. The result connects monkey F5 mirror-neuron physiology to human M1 oscillatory dynamics and validates beta-band desynchronization as a non-invasive readout of action-observation networks.

## Relevance to the review
Direct dependency: ThePresent ERSP analysis spans alpha-beta (mu) bands recorded with the same scalp footprint over rolandic cortex. Hari et al. anchor the prediction that watching the boy or puppy initiate an action at shot onset will reliably suppress 15-25 Hz power over central electrodes, with magnitude proportional to action specificity. They also establish the temporal dynamics (rebound onset ~500 ms post-event) directly relevant to the 0-500 ms ERSP window, and constrain interpretation: any boy-vs-puppy mu-band difference must be evaluated against this generic action-viewing suppression baseline.

## Notable details
- 122-channel Neuromag MEG, 8 healthy adults (4F, 4M; 25-36 yrs).
- Three conditions: rest, manipulate small object, observe experimenter manipulate.
- 15-25 Hz rebound integrated 500-1500 ms post-median-nerve stimulus.
- Observation suppression: 31-46% of execution-related suppression (p < 0.005).
- 7-15 Hz (alpha/mu) also dampened during observation, smaller effect, hemisphere-specific.
- Control: random dot motion produced 16-22% suppression, significantly smaller than action observation (p ~ 0.04).
- No EMG signal during observation, ruling out covert muscle contraction.

## Open questions / limitations
- Hand actions only; generalization to whole-body biological motion (walking, running, the boy and puppy in ThePresent) is untested by this study.
- Adult sample; HBN cohort spans 5-21 years where the action-observation matching system is still developing.
- ~20 Hz rebound is a post-stimulus phenomenon driven by median-nerve pulse; ThePresent has no peripheral stimulation, so the relevant analog is event-related desynchronization (ERD) following shot onsets, not post-stimulus rebound suppression.
- N=8; effect sizes useful but underpowered for individual-subject classification.

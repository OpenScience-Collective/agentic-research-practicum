# Source: Hari et al. 1998, Activation of human primary motor cortex during action observation

- DOI: 10.1073/pnas.95.25.15061
- Journal: Proc Natl Acad Sci USA, 95(25), 15061-15065
- License: PNAS open archive (articles freely available after 6 months); 1998 paper, redistributable.
- PDF archived: source.pdf

## Abstract

The monkey premotor cortex contains neurons that discharge during action execution and during observation of actions made by others. Transcranial magnetic stimulation experiments suggest that a similar observation/execution matching system also is present in humans. We recorded neuromagnetic oscillatory activity of the human precentral cortex from 10 healthy volunteers while (i) they had no task to perform, (ii) they were manipulating a small object, and (iii) they were observing another individual performing the same task. The left and right median nerves were stimulated alternately (interstimulus interval, 1.5 s) at intensities exceeding motor threshold, and the poststimulus rebound of the rolandic 15- to 25-Hz activity was quantified. In agreement with previous studies, the rebound was strongly suppressed bilaterally during object manipulation. Most interestingly, the rebound also was significantly diminished during action observation (31-46% of the suppression during object manipulation). Control experiments, in which subjects were instructed to observe stationary or moving stimuli, confirmed the specificity of the suppression effect. Because the recorded 15- to 25-Hz activity is known to originate mainly in the precentral motor cortex, we concluded that the human primary motor cortex is activated during observation as well as execution of motor tasks.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- Participants: 10 healthy adults, 8 retained (2 lacked reactive rolandic rhythms).
- 122-channel Neuromag MEG; left and right median nerves stimulated at 1.5 s ISI, 7-13 mA exceeding motor threshold.
- Conditions: rest, right-hand object manipulation, observation of experimenter manipulating same object.
- Two control conditions: viewing color-changing LEDs (attention control); viewing a randomly moving dot in a 13x14 cm rectangle (motion control).
- Analysis: temporal spectral evolution (TSE) of band-pass filtered 14-30 Hz (mainly 15-25 Hz) signals integrated 500-1500 ms post-median-nerve stimulus; sources modeled as single current dipoles on individual MRIs.

## Key results

- ~20 Hz rebound suppressed bilaterally during object manipulation by 8.9-14.4 fT/cm depending on side (all p < 0.001).
- Action observation suppressed rebound by 31-46% of the execution-related suppression (p < 0.005), with no significant difference between hemispheres or between LMN and RMN stimulation.
- 7-15 Hz (mu) also dampened during observation (60-88% of the 20-Hz suppression magnitude), significant only in the left hemisphere for LMN stimuli.
- LED viewing produced no rebound change; random-dot motion produced 16-22% suppression, significantly less than action observation.
- Surface EMG showed no muscle activation during observation, ruling out covert motor execution.

## Authors' conclusion

The data support a human observation-execution matching system involving the primary motor cortex, paralleling monkey F5 mirror-neuron physiology, and demonstrate that the rolandic ~20 Hz rebound is a sensitive and specific non-invasive marker of this system.

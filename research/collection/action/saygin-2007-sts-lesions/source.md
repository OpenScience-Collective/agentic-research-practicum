# Source: Saygin 2007, Superior temporal and premotor brain areas necessary for biological motion perception

- DOI: 10.1093/brain/awm162
- Journal: Brain, 130(9), 2452-2461
- Publisher: Oxford University Press
- License: publisher-paywall (OUP); not redistributable.

## Abstract (verbatim from publisher record)

The neural basis of human biological motion perception was investigated using voxel-based lesion-symptom mapping in 60 patients with chronic, unilateral focal brain lesions. Patients performed two tasks involving point-light biological motion stimuli: a detection task and a direction discrimination task. Lesion-deficit analyses controlled for performance on a non-biological motion task. Damage to posterior superior temporal areas and to ventral premotor and inferior frontal cortex was significantly associated with impaired biological motion perception. Additional analyses showed that lesions to ventral premotor cortex impaired detection of biological motion even when posterior superior temporal cortex was intact. The findings provide causal evidence that human biological motion perception relies on a network including both posterior superior temporal cortex and ventral premotor cortex, supporting accounts in which the human action-observation system contributes to perceiving the actions of others.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- 60 chronic unilateral stroke patients; lesion masks coregistered to MNI space.
- Two PLD tasks: detection in scrambled-noise masks, direction (leftward vs rightward) discrimination.
- Non-biological motion control (rigid object motion) regressed out.
- VLSM: per-voxel logistic regression of impairment, permutation-thresholded.

## Key results

- Posterior STS damage predicted biological motion deficits (consistent with prior fMRI).
- Ventral premotor / inferior frontal cortex damage independently predicted deficits.
- vPMC effect held even when STS was intact, providing dissociation evidence.
- No selective deficit from primary visual cortex lesions beyond generic motion impairment.

## Authors' conclusion

Biological motion perception in humans depends causally on a fronto-temporal network: posterior STS and ventral premotor cortex jointly support action perception. The action-execution network is not a passive mirror; it contributes necessary processing.

# Source: Chen, Leong, Honey, Yong, Norman & Hasson 2017, Shared memories reveal shared structure in neural activity across individuals

- DOI: 10.1038/nn.4450
- Journal: Nature Neuroscience, 20(1), 115-125
- Publisher: Springer Nature
- License: publisher-paywall (Springer Nature); PMC author manuscript at PMC5191958.

## Abstract (verbatim)

Our lives revolve around sharing experiences and memories with others. When different people recount the same events, do they organize and represent these events similarly? In this study, we used neuroimaging to understand the neural basis of how individuals encode, store, and retrieve naturalistic, continuous events. Participants watched a 48-minute episode of a TV show during functional MRI, then verbally recounted the episode in detail. We found that high-level brain regions, including default-mode regions, exhibited shared neural patterns across participants during both encoding and retrieval. The shared patterns were event-specific and reactivated during recall. Cross-subject similarity at retrieval predicted memory accuracy, indicating that the more similar event representations were across people, the more likely those events were to be remembered.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- N=17 adult participants.
- Stimulus: 48-minute BBC Sherlock pilot (Episode 1: A Study in Pink).
- Encoding: passive movie viewing during fMRI.
- Retrieval: free verbal recall in scanner immediately afterward.
- Event boundaries identified by independent raters.
- Cross-subject pattern similarity computed per event in default-mode regions.

## Key results

- Default-mode regions (mPFC, posterior cingulate, angular gyrus) show event-specific patterns shared across participants.
- Patterns reinstated during recall: same signature at recall and during original viewing.
- Cross-subject similarity at retrieval correlates with memory accuracy.

## Authors' conclusion

Memories of naturalistic events are organized into shared, event-specific neural representations in default-mode cortex; cross-subject similarity at retrieval is a marker of memory robustness.

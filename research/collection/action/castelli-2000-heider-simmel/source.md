# Source: Castelli, Happe, Frith & Frith 2000, Movement and mind

- DOI: 10.1006/nimg.2000.0612
- Journal: NeuroImage, 12(3), 314-325
- Publisher: Elsevier
- License: publisher-paywall.

## Abstract (verbatim)

We used positron emission tomography (PET) to study the brain mechanisms underlying the perception and interpretation of intentional movement patterns. We presented adult subjects with silent computer-presented animations consisting of two triangles moving about on a screen in three different ways: (a) randomly, (b) in a goal-directed fashion (chasing, fighting), or (c) in a way that could be interpreted as conveying intentional and mentalistic states (e.g., persuading, tricking). Verbal accounts elicited from subjects after each animation indicated that they made systematically different interpretations of the animations: descriptions of random motion involved physical descriptions, descriptions of goal-directed motion involved active terms, and descriptions of ToM motion involved mental-state attributions. Brain activations specifically associated with the ToM animations were localized to medial prefrontal cortex, the temporo-parietal junction, the temporal poles, and the extrastriate cortex.

## Note on imaging modality

The original 2000 paper used PET (the abstract and methods refer to PET); subsequent fMRI replications and extensions are common in the literature. The card slug retains the historically influential paradigm framing.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- N=6 adults.
- Three animation classes: random, goal-directed, theory-of-mind.
- Verbal-description verification of intended interpretations.
- PET contrasts: ToM > random and ToM > goal-directed.

## Key results

- ToM > random activations: medial prefrontal cortex, right pSTS / temporo-parietal junction, bilateral temporal poles, extrastriate cortex.
- Activations driven by interpretation, not low-level motion features.
- Triangles can recruit social-cognition network when motion is mentalistic.

## Authors' conclusion

The social-cognition / theory-of-mind network is engaged by abstract animations of intentional motion, demonstrating that the network operates on inferred agency rather than realistic social form.

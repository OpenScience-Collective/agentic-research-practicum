## Abstract (verbatim)

Real-world events unfold at different time scales, with the meaning of each event depending on the temporal context of preceding events. To characterize the topography of brain regions sensitive to information accumulated over different time scales, we used functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) to record neural responses while subjects listened to a story that was either intact or scrambled at the time scale of paragraphs, sentences, or words. We measured intersubject correlation in each region as a function of stimulus integrity. Within early auditory cortices (e.g., Heschl's gyrus and superior temporal sulcus), responses were similarly reliable across all stimuli. Within higher-order regions (e.g., precuneus, posterior medial frontal cortex, temporal parietal junction), responses became reliable only as the story's long-range temporal structure was preserved. These results indicate that within the cortex, there exists a topographic hierarchy of temporal receptive windows.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- N=36 adult participants.
- Stimulus: 7-minute narrated story.
- Four conditions: intact, paragraph-scrambled, sentence-scrambled, word-scrambled.
- ISC: voxelwise correlation across participants in each condition.

## Key results

- Hierarchy of TRWs across cortex.
- Early auditory cortex correlates in all conditions: short TRW.
- Middle temporal regions: intermediate TRWs (sentences).
- Angular gyrus, temporal-parietal junction, precuneus, posterior medial cortex: long TRWs (paragraphs/full).

## Authors' conclusion

The cortex implements a topographic hierarchy of temporal receptive windows; long-window regions integrate information over tens of seconds, supporting narrative comprehension.

## Abstract (verbatim)

There is a system of neurons in the brain called mirror neurons that are activated when one observes another individual perform a goal-directed action. Mirror neurons are believed to be important for understanding the actions of other people, and for learning new skills by imitation. Mirror neurons are also thought to be important for the development of empathy. This review describes the most relevant studies on mirror neurons in monkeys and humans, focusing on imitation, empathy, and the relationship between mirror neurons and language. The article also discusses the implications of mirror neuron research for understanding the social deficits in autism spectrum disorder.

## Key sections paraphrased

1. **Mirror neurons in monkeys and humans**: F5/PF in macaque; IFG, vPMC, IPL in humans.
2. **Imitation**: pars opercularis (BA 44) and IPL specifically activated during action imitation vs simple observation.
3. **Empathy and emotion**: facial expression observation activates a mirror-emotion network including anterior insula and amygdala via projections from IFG.
4. **Intention understanding**: anterior IPL responses differ for contextually framed grasping actions (drinking vs cleaning).
5. **Autism**: reduced mirror-system activation during emotion imitation; controversial broken-mirror hypothesis discussed.
6. **Language**: links to evolution, gesture-speech overlap.

# Source: Kilner, Friston & Frith 2007, Predictive coding: an account of the mirror neuron system

- DOI: 10.1007/s10339-007-0170-2
- Journal: Cognitive Processing, 8, 159-166
- Publisher: Springer

## Abstract (verbatim)

The mirror-neuron system is widely thought to be involved in action understanding. However, the way in which the mirror-neuron system mediates action understanding has remained the subject of intense debate. We discuss the merits of these debates and propose that they may be resolved by reformulating the mirror-neuron system within a predictive coding framework. We propose that the mirror-neuron system represents an inverse model of the kinematics, goal, and intention of observed actions. From this perspective, action observation entails the suppression of prediction error in a hierarchical, dynamic Bayesian fashion. We discuss this account in light of recent neurophysiological data and consider the implications for understanding the mirror-neuron system at the level of the cortical microcircuitry.

## Theoretical framework

- Three-level Bayesian hierarchy:
  1. **Intention** (highest level): why the agent is acting.
  2. **Goal**: what the agent is trying to achieve (object of action).
  3. **Kinematics** (lowest level): the observed movement trajectories.
- Top-down predictions flow from intention to kinematics; prediction errors flow upward.
- Action understanding = inverting the generative model: given observed kinematics, infer most likely intention.

## Reinterpretation of mirror neurons

- Strictly congruent mirror neurons = kinematic-level prediction units.
- Broadly congruent mirror neurons = goal-level prediction units.
- Activation during observation reflects the predictions or prediction errors at the corresponding hierarchical level, not literal motor simulation.

## Key empirical predictions

- Mirror response magnitude scales with predictability: highly predictable actions should produce smaller responses; surprising actions, larger responses.
- Top-down priors about agent identity or goal should modulate kinematic-level responses.
- Lesions to higher levels (intention) should disrupt action understanding without abolishing kinematic-level mirror responses.

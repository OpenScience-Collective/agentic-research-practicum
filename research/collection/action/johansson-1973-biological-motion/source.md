# Source: Johansson 1973, Visual perception of biological motion and a model for its analysis

## Abstract (verbatim from publisher record)

A method for the study of visual motion perception by means of patterns of moving dots is described. The method implies that 10-12 elements attached to the main joints of a moving subject in dark surroundings carry information sufficient for immediate visual identification of the human motion pattern. A mathematical analysis of motion combinations is presented, together with experimental results. The vector model accounts for the perceptual organization of the points of light into a unified motion percept of an articulated body via decomposition into a common (translatory) component and a residual relative-motion component.

## Key claims captured for paper-card

1. Point-light displays (PLDs) of articulated agents support recognition of biological motion (BM).
2. Inversion abolishes recognition, supporting an upright-specific mechanism.
3. A vector model of relative motions explains how rigid-body and articulated components are parsed.
4. Recognition is fast (<= ~400 ms display) and robust to point-set reductions.

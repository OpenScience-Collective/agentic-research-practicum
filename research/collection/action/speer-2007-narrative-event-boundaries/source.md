# Source: Speer, Zacks & Reynolds 2007, Human brain activity time-locked to narrative event boundaries

- DOI: 10.1111/j.1467-9280.2007.01920.x
- Journal: Psychological Science, 18(5), 449-455
- Publisher: SAGE/Wiley (Association for Psychological Science)
- License: publisher-paywall.

## Abstract (verbatim)

Activity in everyday life is conceptualized as a series of discrete events. Here we investigated whether human brain activity is time-locked to event boundaries when participants engage in narrative comprehension. While in a functional magnetic resonance imaging scanner, participants listened to narrative excerpts. A separate group of participants identified the boundaries between events in the same narratives. Brain activity in posterior brain regions, including the posterior cingulate cortex, the medial parietal lobe, regions in lateral occipital cortex, and the supplementary motor area, was time-locked to event boundaries. Activity was stronger at coarse-grained boundaries than at fine-grained ones. The findings indicate that during passive narrative comprehension the brain identifies event boundaries automatically and that these boundaries play a role in segmenting experience into discrete units.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- 18 adult fMRI participants; passive listening to spoken narrative excerpts.
- Separate behavioral group of raters identified fine and coarse event boundaries.
- BOLD time courses convolved with boundary timings; group-level GLM identified boundary-locked responses.

## Key results

- Posterior cingulate cortex, medial parietal cortex, lateral occipital cortex, supplementary motor area show boundary-locked activations.
- Coarse boundaries produce stronger activations than fine.
- Effects emerge during passive listening (no explicit segmentation task).

## Authors' conclusion

Event-boundary detection is automatic during narrative comprehension; multiple cortical regions index boundary occurrence with hierarchical sensitivity (coarse > fine).

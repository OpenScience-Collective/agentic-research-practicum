- Journal: Psychological Bulletin, 133(2), 273-293
- Publisher: American Psychological Association

## Abstract (verbatim)

People perceive and conceive of activity in terms of discrete events. Here the authors propose a theory according to which the perception of boundaries between events arises from ongoing perceptual processing and regulates attention and memory. Perceptual systems continuously make predictions about what will happen next. When transient errors in predictions arise, an event boundary is perceived. According to the theory, the perception of events depends on both sensory cues and knowledge structures that represent previously learned information about event parts and inferences about actors' goals and plans. Neurological and neurophysiological data suggest that representations of events may be implemented by structures in the lateral prefrontal cortex and that perceptual prediction error is calculated and evaluated by a processing pathway, including the anterior cingulate cortex and subcortical neuromodulatory systems.

## Theoretical core (paraphrased)

- Event Segmentation Theory (EST): observers maintain an "event model" (working representation of current activity), updated by sensory input.
- Prediction error: when sensory input deviates from event-model predictions, error increases.
- Boundary: at peaks of prediction error, the event model is reset, and the observer registers a boundary.
- Hierarchy: fine and coarse boundaries nested; coarse changes in goal/location vs fine changes in motion.
- Memory: information at boundaries preferentially encoded (boundary advantage).

## Key empirical findings reviewed

- Behavioral inter-rater agreement on segmentation tasks ~60-90% across stimuli.
- fMRI: posterior cortical regions (e.g., MT+, posterior parietal, posterior cingulate) show transient activity peaks at boundaries.
- Hierarchical structure recoverable from button-press timings.
- Memory boost for boundary-adjacent content.

## Predictors of boundaries

- Actor change.
- Location change.
- Object change.
- Goal change.
- Causal-continuity break.

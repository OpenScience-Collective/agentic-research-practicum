# Event perception: a mind-brain perspective

## TL;DR
Defines Event Segmentation Theory (EST): observers continuously maintain working "event models" and segment ongoing experience at moments of high prediction error, with event boundaries as discrete cognitive markers that organize perception, memory, and action.

## Summary
Zacks and colleagues review behavioral and neuroimaging evidence that event segmentation is automatic, reliable across observers, and predictive of memory. They formalize EST: an event model is a working representation of the current "what is happening" that is updated incrementally by sensory input. When prediction error spikes, the model resets, and the observer registers an event boundary. Boundaries are reliably marked behaviorally (button-press segmentation tasks; ~60-90% inter-rater agreement) and neurally (fMRI activations in posterior cortex at boundary moments; Speer et al. 2007). They organize information at multiple temporal grains (fine vs coarse). The review surveys evidence from naturalistic narratives (movies, stories) showing that event boundaries align with shifts in actor, object, location, goal, or causal structure, with stronger activations for shifts in multiple dimensions.

## Relevance to the review
Direct dependency: ThePresent shot boundaries are precisely the event boundaries this theory describes. Boy-vs-puppy contrast aligns with boundary moments where the agent on screen changes (a strong predictor of high prediction error per EST). EST predicts that early ERSP responses at shot onsets should reflect the magnitude of model updating; shots that introduce a new agent or substantially shift the agent's role should produce larger event-boundary signals than shots that continue the current model. This is the conceptual scaffolding that lets a per-shot ERSP analysis be interpreted as event-perception research, not just stimulus-driven sensory analysis.

## Notable details
- Behavioral segmentation: ~60-90% agreement among observers; reliable across days.
- fMRI evidence (Zacks 2001, Speer 2007): posterior cortical activity peaks at event boundaries.
- Multi-grain hierarchy: fine-grained boundaries nested within coarse-grained.
- Boundary predictors: changes in actor, location, goal, object, causal continuity.
- Memory consequence: information at event boundaries better remembered (boundary advantage).
- Theoretical claim: prediction error drives boundary detection; event model is a top-down generative model.

## Open questions / limitations
- Pre-2010 review; predates HMM-based methods (Baldassano 2017) for inferring boundaries from neural data alone.
- Behavioral segmentation literature uses adult samples; HBN cohort spans age, with developmentally varying segmentation reliability (Kurby & Zacks 2008 for aging is on the other end).
- Movie-style cuts (the explicit shot boundaries in ThePresent) are visually abrupt; the theory's prediction-error account for sudden visual change vs gradual narrative transition needs separation.
- Doesn't directly speak to oscillatory dynamics; the EEG/MEG literature on event-boundary signals is sparse and post-2007.

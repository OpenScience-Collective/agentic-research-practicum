# Source: Klin et al. 2009, Two-year-olds with autism orient to nonsocial contingencies rather than biological motion

- DOI: 10.1038/nature07868
- Journal: Nature, 459, 257-261 (PMID 19329996, PMC2758571)
- Publisher: Springer Nature
- License: publisher-paywall (NPG); PMC has author manuscript text but not redistributable as journal-formatted PDF without author permission.

## Abstract (verbatim from publisher record)

Typically developing human infants preferentially attend to biological motion within the first days of life. This ability is highly conserved across species and is critical to filial attachment and social development. The neuroanatomical foundations and cognitive operations underlying this trait are poorly understood, however. Here we show that two-year-olds with autism fail to orient towards point-light displays of biological motion, and their viewing patterns are instead driven by the presence of nonsocial, physical contingencies that are disregarded by control children. Despite their failure to attend to biological motion, autistic children are highly sensitive to the presence of audiovisual synchrony. Our results offer a new mechanistic basis for the early social orienting impairments observed in autism, and provide an empirical method for the early detection of autism, a critical step in lowering the age at which the disorder is diagnosed and treatments begin.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- Three groups: 21 children with autism, 16 typically developing (TD), 16 with developmental delay (DD); mean age ~24 months.
- Side-by-side preferential-looking eye-tracking task: upright PLD walker vs inverted/scrambled walker, audio paired with upright walker (footstep sounds in step-time).
- Five trials per pairing; gaze duration measured in each AOI (area of interest).
- Follow-up manipulation: re-paired audio with inverted walker to test whether audiovisual contingency, not biological motion per se, drove autism gaze.

## Key results

- TD: 70% looking time on upright walker, robust biological-motion preference.
- DD: similar preference, 67%.
- Autism: no preference for upright walker; near-chance.
- When audio synchrony was re-paired with inverted walker, autism children oriented to inverted walker; TD/DD did not.
- Conclusion: autism gaze is captured by audiovisual contingency rather than biological-agent kinematics.

## Authors' conclusion

Early social-orienting impairment in autism is not absence of attention but rather orientation toward physical contingencies, providing a candidate behavioral marker for early autism detection.

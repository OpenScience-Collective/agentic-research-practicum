# Source: Pineda 2005, The functional significance of mu rhythms

- DOI: 10.1016/j.brainresrev.2005.04.005
- Journal: Brain Research Reviews, 50(1), 57-68
- Publisher: Elsevier
- License: publisher-paywall.

## Abstract (verbatim)

The mu rhythm is one of three rhythms recorded over Rolandic cortex that has its peak between 8 and 13 Hz. Like the visual alpha rhythm, mu suppresses or desynchronizes when the underlying cortex engages in active processing, but it has a quite different functional profile. As reviewed here, the available evidence indicates that mu suppression accompanies not only the performance of motor acts but also the observation, the imagination, and even the verbal description of motor acts. This convergence is similar to what has been described for "mirror neurons" in monkey and human cortex. We propose that mu rhythms reflect downstream modulation of primary sensorimotor areas by mirror neuron activity, and represent an important means of translating "seeing" and "hearing" into "doing." Mu suppression may be a useful tool for studying mirror neuron functioning in healthy and pathological populations.

## Topics covered (paraphrased)

1. **Anatomy of mu**: 8-13 Hz, distinct from posterior alpha; sources in central sulcus, primary motor and somatosensory cortex.
2. **Functional reactivity**: suppressed by execution, observation, imagery, action-related auditory perception, and verbal description of action.
3. **Mirror-neuron hypothesis**: mu suppression reflects sensorimotor cortical activation by mirror-system feedback.
4. **Developmental trajectory**: present in infancy, refines through childhood.
5. **Clinical applications**: autism (Oberman et al. 2005 reduced mu suppression), schizophrenia, motor disorders.
6. **Methodological recommendations**: individualized mu frequency, log-ratio normalization, C3/Cz/C4 electrode pooling.

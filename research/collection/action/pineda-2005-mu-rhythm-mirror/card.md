# The functional significance of mu rhythms: translating "seeing" and "hearing" into "doing"

## TL;DR
Review consolidating EEG evidence that the mu rhythm (8-13 Hz over sensorimotor cortex) suppresses during action observation, action-related auditory perception, and motor imagery, arguing mu suppression is a non-invasive proxy for the mirror-neuron system in humans.

## Summary
Pineda surveys the EEG mu-rhythm literature from the 1950s onward, arguing that mu (alpha-band activity over central sensorimotor sites, electrodes C3/Cz/C4) is functionally distinct from posterior alpha. Mu suppresses during voluntary movement (long known) but also during action observation (Cochin et al. 1999), action-related auditory perception (Galantucci et al. 2006), and motor imagery. Pineda interprets this consistent suppression pattern as evidence that mu reflects sensorimotor cortical activation by an integrative mirror-neuron-like system that maps perceived events into motor representations. He reviews lifespan data showing mu suppression in infants (3-month-olds) increases through childhood. Methodological recommendations: use individual mu-frequency identification, recommend C3/Cz/C4 pooling, baseline normalization. Frames mu suppression as the most accessible non-invasive readout of action-observation circuit engagement.

## Relevance to the review
Direct dependency for ERSP analysis: mu (~8-13 Hz) is the canonical EEG signature of the action-observation matching system over central electrodes, and the 0-500 ms window in ThePresent shot-onset analysis covers the early phase of mu event-related desynchronization (ERD). The boy-vs-puppy contrast directly tests whether mu suppression is modulated by the identity of the agent initiating action; Pineda's review provides the theoretical scaffolding to predict mu effects and the methodological scaffolding for individualized mu frequency selection. Note that this is review-level; for direct quantitative predictions one needs primary data such as Hari 1998 and Oberman 2007.

## Notable details
- Mu rhythm: 8-13 Hz over C3/Cz/C4; distinct from posterior alpha by topography and reactivity.
- Suppressed by execution, observation, imagery, and action-related auditory perception.
- Developmental trajectory: present in infancy, refines through childhood to adult-like by ~10 years (per studies cited).
- Recommended analysis: log-ratio of power during task vs baseline; individualized mu frequency.
- Mu may have multiple sub-bands (lower mu ~8-10 Hz, upper mu ~10-13 Hz) with somewhat different functional profiles.

## Open questions / limitations
- Pre-2010 review: predates large EEG-fMRI integration showing mu sources include both M1/S1 and IPL/IFG (Arnstein et al. 2011).
- Mu suppression is necessary but not sufficient for mirror-neuron interpretation (Hobson & Bishop 2017 critique posterior alpha contamination).
- Adult-centric review with developmental claims based on small N studies; HBN cohort 5-21 yrs may show developmentally varying mu reactivity.
- No discussion of beta-band mirror responses (covered separately by Hari 1998).

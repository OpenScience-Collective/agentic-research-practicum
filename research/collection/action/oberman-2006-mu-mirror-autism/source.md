- Journal: Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience, 2(1), 62-66
- Publisher: Oxford University Press

## Abstract (verbatim)

Mirror neurons are a class of neurons, originally identified in macaque monkeys, that respond both when an individual performs an action and when they observe a similar action performed by another. In humans, mu rhythm suppression during action observation has been implicated as a measure of the activity of a mirror neuron system. The current study investigated the relationship between mu suppression and various social abilities in normally functioning adults. Participants observed videos of human hand grasping movements and a static-hand control, while EEG was recorded over sensorimotor regions. Mu suppression to observed action was correlated with self-report measures of social functioning: stronger mu suppression accompanied higher empathy quotient and social-skill scores, and lower autism-spectrum quotient scores. These findings support the hypothesis that the mirror neuron system, as indexed by EEG mu suppression, plays a role in social cognition in the typically developing population.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- 27 neurotypical adult participants.
- Stimuli: video clips of hand grasping; static hand control.
- EEG recording at C3, Cz, C4 (and others).
- Mu suppression: log power ratio of 8-13 Hz, observation vs control.
- Self-report: Social Skills Inventory (SSI), Empathy Quotient (EQ), Autism Spectrum Quotient (AQ).

## Key results

- Mu suppression correlates positively with EQ and SSI; negatively with AQ.
- Effect strongest at C3/C4; central topography consistent with mu source.
- Static-hand control did not produce mu suppression.

## Authors' conclusion

Mu suppression to action observation co-varies with social-cognitive ability across neurotypical adults, supporting the mirror system as a substrate for social cognition.

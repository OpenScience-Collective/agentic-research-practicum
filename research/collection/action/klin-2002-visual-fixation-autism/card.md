# Visual fixation patterns during viewing of naturalistic social situations as predictors of social competence in individuals with autism

## TL;DR
Adolescents and young adults with autism, while watching emotionally evocative live-action movie scenes (Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?), fixate the eyes of social agents 50% as often as typically developing controls and the mouth twice as often, with eye-fixation time strongly predicting social competence on autism diagnostic instruments.

## Summary
Klin and colleagues recorded eye position from 15 adolescents/young adults with autism and 15 typically developing controls while viewing dynamic film clips with intense social content (largely from "Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?"). Quantification of fixations on four areas of interest (eyes, mouth, body, objects) showed a striking dissociation: TD viewers preferentially fixated eyes; autism viewers preferentially fixated mouths and physical objects. Eye-fixation time correlated negatively with autism severity (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule scores). The result establishes that gaze allocation during naturalistic social-movie viewing is a sensitive behavioral marker of social-competence individual differences. Foundational for the Pixar / movie-watching paradigm in pediatric autism eye tracking that informs pediatric naturalistic neuroimaging.

## Relevance to the review
Medium relevance: the HBN cohort includes participants across the autism spectrum. ThePresent involves boy-puppy gaze and emotional dynamics. Klin et al. predict that fixation patterns within shots will differ across the cohort (eyes vs mouth fixation), and these visual-attention differences may propagate to early ERSP responses. The paper supports inclusion of social-cognition individual-differences measures (e.g., SRS) as moderators of the boy-puppy contrast. Also a foundational naturalistic-movie paradigm that anticipates the use of Pixar shorts (like ThePresent) in pediatric neuroimaging.

## Notable details
- N=15 autism (mean age ~17 yr), N=15 TD controls.
- Stimuli: clips from "Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?" with naturalistic dialogue and social interaction.
- Four AOIs: eyes, mouth, body, object.
- Autism: 50% as much eye fixation as TD; 2x as much mouth fixation.
- Eye fixation time correlates with social competence (ADOS) negatively.
- Object fixation positively correlated with autism severity.

## Open questions / limitations
- Adolescent / young adult autism sample; younger HBN participants may show different fixation patterns.
- Live-action movie with adult dialogue; ThePresent is animated, no dialogue, child-oriented; gaze patterns may differ.
- No EEG; says nothing about ERSP. Provides behavioral grounding.
- Pre-current diagnostic criteria (DSM-IV-era autism); may not generalize directly to broader spectrum.

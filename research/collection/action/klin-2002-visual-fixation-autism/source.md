# Source: Klin, Jones, Schultz, Volkmar & Cohen 2002, Visual fixation patterns during viewing of naturalistic social situations as predictors of social competence in individuals with autism

- DOI: 10.1001/archpsyc.59.9.809
- Journal: Archives of General Psychiatry, 59(9), 809-816
- Publisher: AMA (now JAMA Psychiatry)
- License: publisher-paywall (JAMA Network).

## Abstract (verbatim)

Background: A growing body of research suggests that individuals with autism allocate their visual attention atypically. We investigated whether visual fixation patterns during the viewing of naturalistic social situations differ between adolescents and adults with autism and matched comparison subjects, and how such patterns predict social competence.

Methods: Eye tracking was used to measure visual fixation patterns in 15 cognitively able autistic males and 15 typically developing controls watching emotionally engaging clips from "Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?". Four regions of interest were defined: eyes, mouth, body, and objects.

Results: Compared with typically developing peers, autistic individuals showed reduced fixation on eyes (about 50% of TD) and increased fixation on mouths and objects. Eye fixation time strongly predicted social competence as measured by the Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule (ADOS): more eye fixation predicted better social competence.

Conclusions: Visual fixation patterns during naturalistic-movie viewing reveal autism-specific gaze allocation and predict social-competence individual differences.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- N=30 (15 autism, 15 TD), male, adolescent and young adult.
- Stimulus: dialogue clips from "Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?".
- Eye tracking with infrared corneal reflection, 60 Hz sampling.
- AOIs: eyes, mouth, body, object; percent fixation time computed.

## Key results

- Autism: ~50% less eye fixation than TD.
- Autism: 2x more mouth fixation than TD.
- Object fixation higher in autism, correlated with ADOS severity.
- Eye-fixation time predictive of social competence on ADOS.

## Authors' conclusion

Naturalistic-movie eye tracking provides a sensitive marker of autism-specific gaze allocation and individual differences in social competence.

# Source: Sliwa & Freiwald 2017, A dedicated network for social interaction processing in the primate brain

- DOI: 10.1126/science.aam6383
- Journal: Science, 356(6339), 745-749
- Publisher: AAAS
- License: publisher-paywall (Science); PMC author manuscript at PMC5540642 may exist.

## Abstract (verbatim)

Primate behavior is uniquely shaped by social interactions, but how the brain encodes social information has remained unclear. We trained macaques to watch movies of physical interactions between agents, single-agent actions, and interactions between objects, and we used functional magnetic resonance imaging to map activations across the brain. We found a network of regions, located in the superior temporal sulcus and medial prefrontal cortex, that responded selectively to social interactions but not to single-agent actions or object interactions. The selectivity could not be explained by attention or low-level visual features. Together with prior human evidence, our results suggest that the primate brain contains a dedicated network for the processing of social interactions.

## Methods (paraphrased)

- 4 awake macaques.
- Stimulus categories: social interactions (two macaques interacting), single-agent actions (one macaque acting), object interactions (objects only).
- Whole-brain fMRI with monocrystalline iron oxide nanoparticle (MION) contrast.
- Voxelwise contrasts identified social-interaction-selective network.

## Key results

- Posterior STS, anterior STS, and medial prefrontal cortex respond selectively to social interactions.
- Selectivity holds after controlling for low-level visual features.
- Network parallels human social-interaction-selective regions reported in earlier literature.

## Authors' conclusion

A dedicated cortical network encodes observed social interactions in the primate brain, distinct from single-agent action processing and from object-interaction processing.

## TL;DR

Mothers viewing photographs of their *own* child and their *own* dog activate a partly shared affiliative network, with reward-system midbrain (ventral tegmental area / substantia nigra, VTA/SN) selectively engaged for the child and posterior cortex (fusiform gyrus, FG) selectively engaged for the dog.

## Summary

Stoeckel and colleagues scanned 14 mothers viewing four photo sets, own child, unfamiliar child, own dog, unfamiliar dog. Common ("own > unfamiliar") activation across child and dog spanned regions implicated in emotion, reward, affiliation, visual processing, and social cognition. Differences were specific: own-child > own-dog elicited stronger VTA/SN response (a canonical reward/parental-attachment region), while own-dog > own-child elicited stronger fusiform gyrus response. Subjective ratings of arousal and valence were similar across own-child and own-dog images, but the own-versus-unfamiliar arousal contrast was larger for the child than the dog. Valence ratings of the dog correlated with self-reported attachment to the dog.

## Relevance to the review

This is the load-bearing primary source for Category 5 (pet/animal-evoked affect) in the boy-shot vs puppy-shot contrast. Critically, the contrast is *own-child versus own-dog in mothers*, not generic "baby versus puppy" face perception, so any synthesis must avoid mis-framing it. The shared affiliative network (caudate, periaqueductal gray, medial prefrontal cortex) supports the premise that boy-shot and puppy-shot frames in The Present should both engage social/affective regions, though the differentiating structures (VTA/SN for child, FG for dog) suggest dissociable contributions even in a child cohort.

Developmental-justification rule: this is an adult-only study (mothers, mean age 41). Direct transfer to ages 5-21 (Healthy Brain Network, HBN, cohort) is not given. The maternal pair-bond circuitry (oxytocin-rich VTA/SN response to own child) is unlikely to drive child-viewer responses to The Present's boy. The fusiform-for-dog effect, however, is a perceptual category response that should generalize across ages once face/object processing matures (>= ~6 years). Mark the developmental gap as an open question on the synthesis card.

## Notable details

- N = 14 mothers, all childbearing, with at least one child aged 2-10 years and one dog at home for >= 2 years.
- Stimulus set: 4 categories of photos, custom-made for each participant (own child, own dog, unfamiliar child, unfamiliar dog).
- VTA/SN, caudate, periaqueductal gray (PAG), thalamus, and medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) appeared in the conjunction (own-child + own-dog versus unfamiliar).
- Child-specific: VTA/SN; dog-specific: fusiform gyrus, superior temporal gyrus.
- Behavioral: own-child arousal ratings significantly exceeded unfamiliar-child arousal; own-dog versus unfamiliar-dog arousal difference was smaller, hinting at a partly attenuated affective response to dogs.

## Open questions / limitations

- Adult-only sample; no developmental data.
- Static photographs, not naturalistic moving stimuli; The Present is a moving short.
- Own-versus-unfamiliar manipulation is identity-level; no information about a *generic* puppy response (where there is no prior bond).
- N = 14 limits power for region-by-condition interaction effects.

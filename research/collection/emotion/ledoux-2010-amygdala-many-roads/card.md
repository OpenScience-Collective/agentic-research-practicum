## TL;DR

Pessoa and Adolphs argue that the amygdala does not detect threat via a "low-road" subcortical shortcut as classically depicted; instead, multiple cortical and subcortical pathways converge to allow flexible evaluation of biological significance, with substantial cortical contribution.

## Summary

This Nature Reviews Neuroscience perspective revisits the LeDoux "low-road" model, in which subcortical visual information is rapidly routed via thalamus to amygdala for fast threat detection. Reviewing decades of human and animal evidence, Pessoa and Adolphs argue the empirical support for a privileged subcortical pathway is weak: the amygdala receives slow, primarily cortical input; lesions of cortex impair amygdala-mediated emotional processing; and "many roads" rather than one privileged route convey emotionally relevant information. The amygdala should be considered a flexible evaluator of biological significance integrating diverse cortical inputs.

## Relevance to the review

Important caveat for the boy-shot vs puppy-shot interpretation. If early ERSP differences (0-500 ms) appear, a tempting narrative is "fast amygdala-driven affective response". Pessoa and Adolphs caution that fast emotional responses likely involve cortical processing rather than a clean subcortical shortcut, which constrains the mechanistic interpretation of any early ERSP effects. Marked `relevance: medium` because it informs but does not constrain the specific contrast.

Developmental-justification rule: review draws primarily on adult animal and human data. Amygdala connectivity matures across childhood and adolescence (Gee 2013, Tottenham 2009); the Pessoa-Adolphs many-roads framework is consistent with that, but no specific developmental claim is made here. Mark `relevance: medium`.

Note: this entry was originally seeded as "LeDoux amygdala canonical reviews"; the most up-to-date and most-cited canonical review on amygdala emotional processing is Pessoa & Adolphs 2010 NRN ("Emotion processing and the amygdala: from a 'low road' to 'many roads' of evaluating biological significance"), which directly argues against the LeDoux low-road framing. We therefore use this as the canonical review; LeDoux's own 1996 and 2000 reviews remain foundational but predate the multi-pathway debate.

## Notable details

- Reviews ~150 papers across human and animal literatures.
- Anatomical evidence: thalamus-amygdala projections in primates are weaker than implied by the rodent low-road model.
- Cortical lesions disrupt amygdala-mediated processing of complex stimuli.
- Functional flexibility: amygdala responds to a wide range of biologically significant content (positive and negative).
- Published in Nature Reviews Neuroscience, November 2010.

## Open questions / limitations

- Review, no new data.
- Continues an ongoing controversy; LeDoux and others have published rejoinders.
- Limited explicit developmental scope.

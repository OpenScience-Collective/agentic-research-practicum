# Emotion processing and the amygdala: from a 'low road' to 'many roads' of evaluating biological significance

## Citation

Pessoa, L., & Adolphs, R. (2010). Emotion processing and the amygdala: from a 'low road' to 'many roads' of evaluating biological significance. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 11(11), 773-783. https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn2920

## Open access

PMC: PMC3025529 (full text freely readable; not redistributable).

## Abstract (publisher-provided)

The amygdala has long been linked with emotion - the implicit assumption being that it processes affective information independently of cognition. Recent research challenges this view. We propose that the amygdala does not encode emotion per se; instead, it functions to evaluate the biological significance of stimuli, integrating multiple inputs from many sensory and cognitive systems. We review evidence from anatomy, physiology and human neuroimaging arguing that the amygdala receives most of its visual input from cortex, and that even in cases where it appears to process affective stimuli rapidly, this depends on processing in cortical areas. We argue that the amygdala receives information about the emotional significance of stimuli through "many roads" - cortical and subcortical - and that its role is to flexibly evaluate biological significance.

## Note on the seed list

The seed list referenced "LeDoux amygdala canonical reviews". Joseph LeDoux's own canonical reviews (LeDoux 1996 *The Emotional Brain*; LeDoux 2000 *Annu Rev Neurosci* "Emotion circuits in the brain", DOI 10.1146/annurev.neuro.23.1.155) remain widely cited. The Pessoa & Adolphs 2010 paper here is the most-cited modern canonical review that *re-evaluates* LeDoux's framework and provides current empirical context, which is why it is used as the primary entry. The LeDoux 2000 Annu Rev Neurosci paper is included in this paper-card's related works.

## TL;DR

Watching a loved one receive a painful stimulus activates the *affective* (anterior insula, anterior cingulate cortex) but not the *sensory* (somatosensory cortex) components of the pain network in the observer, providing direct evidence that empathy for pain shares the affective-pain machinery with first-person pain.

## Summary

Singer and colleagues used functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) to scan 16 women while their romantic partner received either a painful or non-painful electrical stimulus visible to them via cue. Receiving pain themselves activated the full pain network (anterior insula, anterior cingulate cortex, somatosensory cortex). Observing the partner receive pain activated the affective-pain components (anterior insula, anterior cingulate cortex) but not the sensory components. The dissociation supports a "shared affective representation" account of empathy in which observers re-deploy parts of their own pain machinery to model another person's affective experience.

## Relevance to the review

Foundational paper for Category 7 empathy work. The Present contains moments when the boy is visibly distressed (the puppy bumps into objects; the boy is depicted as initially closed-off about his prosthetic leg). If HBN viewers empathise with the boy's distress, this paper predicts engagement of anterior insula and anterior cingulate cortex independently of any first-person pain. EEG independent components mapped to insula-/ACC-adjacent dipoles are candidate sources for empathic-distress signals during boy-shot frames involving observable boy distress, which could confound or augment the boy-shot vs puppy-shot contrast. Marked `relevance: medium`.

Developmental-justification rule: adult sample (women, mean ~26 y). Empathy-for-pain in children has been studied (Decety 2008, 2010) and shows similar but developmentally graded effects. Mark `relevance: medium` and document gap.

## Notable details

- N = 16 healthy adult women; partners present.
- Electrical pain stimulus to participant or partner.
- Affective pain network: anterior insula, anterior cingulate cortex.
- Sensory pain network (somatosensory) not engaged when observing partner pain.
- Dissociation supports shared-affect model of empathy.

## Open questions / limitations

- Adult-only, all-female sample.
- Romantic-partner paradigm; less clear how generalisation to other social agents (children, characters in film) works.
- Affective vs sensory dissociation has been refined by subsequent multivariate work (Krishnan 2016).
- Does not address valence of the empathic experience (pain only).

## TL;DR

Schmalzle and Grall articulate a media-psychology framework in which "captivating" narrative content drives shared neural responses across audience members, situating inter-subject correlation (ISC) as an objective neural index of audience captivation that complements behavioural and self-report measures of engagement.

## Summary

This Journal of Media Psychology piece reviews the inter-subject correlation (ISC) literature from a media-psychology perspective. Schmalzle and Grall argue that ISC-elevating film content shares features (narrative coherence, emotional intensity, attention-grabbing audiovisual structure) consistent with classical accounts of media captivation. They synthesise functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and electroencephalography (EEG) ISC studies to argue that captivation can be operationalised as the proportion of audience members whose brain time courses are aligned, providing a target metric for media research and content design.

## Relevance to the review

Companion theoretical piece for Cohen 2018 in Category 4. Frames the boy-shot vs puppy-shot contrast within a broader media-psychology question about *what* in narrative content drives shared neural states. The Present is a clear "captivating" stimulus with strong narrative coupling; if the boy and puppy contrast differ in captivation strength, ISC analyses can in principle be condition-stratified.

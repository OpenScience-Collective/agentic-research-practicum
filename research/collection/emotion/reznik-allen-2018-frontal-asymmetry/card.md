## TL;DR

Reznik and Allen update the frontal alpha asymmetry literature 14 years after Coan and Allen 2004, concluding that the evidence base supports a moderator role for trait asymmetry in emotion-relevant outcomes but with smaller and more conditional effects than the original framework implied, and substantial concerns about test-retest reliability and statistical-power issues remain unresolved.

## Summary

This Psychophysiology review revisits the frontal alpha asymmetry framework after more than a decade of subsequent empirical and meta-analytic work. The authors find that while a moderator role for trait asymmetry survives, the effect sizes are smaller, the reliability of single-session measurements is lower, and the relationship to depression in particular has been undermined by van der Vinne 2017. Reznik and Allen propose stricter methodological standards: longer recording sessions, multi-session aggregation, attention to montage and reference choice, and pre-registration to address publication bias.

## Relevance to the review

Companion methodological caution for any frontal-alpha-asymmetry analysis in the HBN ERSP project. Even if frontal-alpha differences appear between boy and puppy conditions, the small effect sizes and reliability concerns documented here should constrain interpretation. Marked `relevance: medium`.

## Notable details

- Updated review 14 years after Coan & Allen 2004.
- Concedes that effect sizes are smaller than originally claimed.
- Reliability of single-session asymmetry is low.
- Recommends multi-session aggregation, longer recordings.
- Encourages pre-registration to address publication bias.

## Open questions / limitations

- Review, no new data.
- Continues to debate measurement and statistical issues without resolving them.
- Does not provide specific recommendations for short-window (<1 s) asymmetry as in HBN's 0-500 ms window.

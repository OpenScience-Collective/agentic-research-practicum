# Reading fiction and reading minds: the role of simulation in the default network

## Citation

Tamir, D. I., Bricker, A. B., Dodell-Feder, D., & Mitchell, J. P. (2016). Reading fiction and reading minds: the role of simulation in the default network. *Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience*, 11(2), 215-224. https://doi.org/10.1093/scan/nsv114

## Open access

PMC: PMC4733342 (NIH author manuscript, freely readable; not redistributable).

## Abstract (publisher-provided)

Fiction is thought to engage the same brain regions that simulate real social experiences. We test this hypothesis by examining whether reading fictional, compared with non-fictional, narratives engages the default network. Twenty-two healthy adult participants read short fictional and non-fictional passages during fMRI. Fictional passages elicited stronger activity than non-fictional passages in default-network regions including medial prefrontal cortex, the posterior cingulate / precuneus, and the temporo-parietal junction. Individual differences in fiction-reading habits predicted activation strength in these regions. The findings support a simulation account: fiction recruits the same brain systems that support thinking about other minds.

## TL;DR

Reading fictional passages, compared with non-fictional matched controls, preferentially engages the default-mode network (DMN), and lifetime exposure to fiction predicts stronger DMN activation during reading, consistent with a simulation-based account of fiction's effect on social cognition.

## Summary

Tamir, Bricker, Dodell-Feder, and Mitchell scanned adult participants reading short literary excerpts that were either fictional or non-fictional, controlled for length, vocabulary, and content. Fiction preferentially activated the default-mode network including medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate / precuneus, and bilateral temporo-parietal junction. Individual differences in lifetime fiction exposure (assessed via author-recognition test) predicted DMN activation magnitude. The authors interpret the results as supporting a simulation account: fiction prompts readers to mentally simulate the experiences of characters, an activity supported by the same DMN/ToM machinery that supports thinking about real others.

## Relevance to the review

Companion paper for Mar 2011 in Category 7. Adds individual-differences evidence: people with more fiction exposure show stronger mentalising-network engagement during narrative consumption. For HBN ages 5-21, lifetime narrative-fiction exposure varies enormously and may be a moderating variable for the boy-shot mentalising effect. Marked `relevance: medium` because the modality (reading) and content (text) differ from The Present (animated film), but the simulation-account framework transfers.

Developmental-justification rule: adult-only sample. Lifetime-fiction predictor itself does not generalise neatly to a 5-year-old. Mark `relevance: medium` and document gap.

## Notable details

- Adult sample (N ~ 22).
- Stimulus: short literary passages, fiction vs non-fiction matched.
- DMN preferentially activated by fiction.
- Lifetime fiction exposure (author-recognition test) predicts DMN activation.
- Argues for simulation as the mechanism by which fiction trains social cognition.

## Open questions / limitations

- Adult-only.
- Reading-based, not film-based.
- Short passages, not full narratives.
- Author-recognition test may track verbal ability rather than fiction exposure per se.

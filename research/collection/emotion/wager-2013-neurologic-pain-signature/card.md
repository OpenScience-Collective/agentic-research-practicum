## TL;DR

Wager and colleagues derived a multivariate brain-activity pattern (the "neurologic pain signature", NPS) from functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) that sensitively and specifically tracks somatic pain across studies, demonstrating the feasibility of stable distributed signatures for affect-related brain states.

## Summary

The authors trained a brain-wide multivariate classifier on fMRI responses to noxious thermal stimulation in healthy adults and validated the resulting neurologic pain signature (NPS) on independent samples. The NPS was sensitive (>90% accuracy) for noxious versus warm stimulation and specific (>90% accuracy) versus social rejection pain at matched intensity. The NPS spans canonical pain regions (anterior insula, anterior cingulate cortex, secondary somatosensory cortex, thalamus) but extracts a multivariate weighting that outperforms univariate analyses. The result establishes brain-signature methods as a viable approach to indexing affect-related brain states.

## Notable details

- N = 114 healthy adults across four studies.
- Noxious heat (thermal) stimulation paradigm.
- Cross-validated NPS achieved >90% sensitivity and specificity on held-out data.
- Distinguishes physical pain from social rejection pain.
- Established the "brain signature" methodology widely applied since.

## Open questions / limitations

- Adult-only.
- Pain, not affect more broadly (though companion work has extended to negative-affect signatures).
- Requires large training sets that are infeasible for many naturalistic-stimulus designs.
- EEG analogue of the NPS is non-trivial owing to spatial resolution differences.

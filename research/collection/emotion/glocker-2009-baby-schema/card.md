## TL;DR

Manipulating the "baby schema" (Kindchenschema, large eyes, round face, high forehead) in infant photographs parametrically modulates nucleus accumbens (NAcc) activation in nulliparous women, demonstrating that cute features recruit the brain's mesolimbic reward system independent of parental status.

## Summary

Glocker and colleagues morphed neutral infant face photographs along the baby-schema axis to produce high, unmanipulated, and low cuteness versions. Sixteen nulliparous women rated the photographs and underwent functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) while viewing them. Behavioral cuteness ratings tracked baby-schema strength as expected. fMRI revealed parametric NAcc activation increasing with baby-schema strength, alongside premotor and anterior cingulate engagement. The authors interpret NAcc activity as a key node of the caregiving motivational system that responds to infantile facial features regardless of caregiving experience, providing a neural substrate for Lorenz's ethological "Kindchenschema" hypothesis.

## Relevance to the review

This is a foundational paper for the puppy-shot interpretation. The puppy in The Present has exaggerated infantile features (large eyes, round head) typical of bred companion dogs; baby schema in animal faces is known to elicit cuteness responses analogous to those for human infants (Borgi 2014, Glocker 2009). If the boy-shot vs puppy-shot ERSP contrast reveals reward-related differences, this paper anchors the neural circuit (NAcc and mesolimbic dopamine) most likely involved.

Developmental-justification rule: study sample is adult women aged 19-26, nulliparous. NAcc/ventral striatum response to cuteness is observed across the lifespan; caregiving-relevant responses to infants emerge before adulthood (e.g., Glocker's later work on adolescents shows similar effects). For HBN ages 5-21, ventral striatal sensitivity to cute faces is plausible but not directly demonstrated, and the developmental trajectory of baby-schema reward sensitivity is itself an open empirical question, especially because NAcc dopaminergic systems undergo prolonged maturation through adolescence. Flag as `relevance: high` because the mechanism is central to the puppy-shot prediction, but document the developmental gap explicitly.

## Notable details

- N = 16 nulliparous women, ages 19-26.
- Stimulus: 60 neutral infant photographs morphed into low, unmanipulated, and high baby-schema variants.
- Parametric NAcc effect: baby-schema strength as a continuous regressor produced a linear increase in NAcc beta.
- No parental experience required; effect generalises beyond mothers.
- Connects ethological theory (Lorenz Kindchenschema) to mesolimbic reward circuitry.

## Open questions / limitations

- All-female sample; sex-specific responses to infant features are documented but not addressed here.
- Adult-only; no direct developmental comparison.
- Static photographs in a slow event-related design, not naturalistic moving stimuli.
- Baby schema in animal faces (puppy) is not tested here; bridging requires Borgi 2014 or analogous animal-stimulus work.

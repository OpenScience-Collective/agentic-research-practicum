# Baby schema modulates the brain reward system in nulliparous women

## Citation

Glocker, M. L., Langleben, D. D., Ruparel, K., Loughead, J. W., Valdez, J. N., Griffin, M. D., Sachser, N., & Gur, R. C. (2009). Baby schema modulates the brain reward system in nulliparous women. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 106(22), 9115-9119. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0811620106

## Open access

PMC: PMC2690007 (full text freely readable but not redistributable per PNAS pre-2017 licensing).

## Abstract (publisher-provided)

Ethologist Konrad Lorenz proposed that baby schema ("Kindchenschema") is a set of infantile physical features such as the large head, round face and big eyes that is perceived as cute and motivates caretaking behavior in other individuals, with the evolutionary function of enhancing offspring survival. Previous work on this fundamental concept was restricted to schematic baby representations or correlations of subjective cuteness ratings with selected facial attributes. Here, we experimentally tested whether baby schema modulates the perception of cuteness and the motivation for caretaking in adults exposed to baby faces and how it engages the mesocorticolimbic system that mediates reward processing. We parametrically manipulated baby schema in photographs of infant faces and measured cuteness perception, motivation for caretaking, and functional magnetic resonance imaging responses in nulliparous women. High baby schema infant faces elicited stronger cuteness perception, motivation for caretaking, and activation of the nucleus accumbens, a key structure of the mesocorticolimbic system mediating reward processing and appetitive motivation. We propose that baby schema activates a brain network for parental caretaking, the engagement of which results in cuteness perception and motivation for caretaking that ultimately serves the goal of offspring survival.

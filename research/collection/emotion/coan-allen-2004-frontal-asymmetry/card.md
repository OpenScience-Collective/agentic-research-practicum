## TL;DR

Coan and Allen distinguish *trait* frontal alpha asymmetry (a dispositional capability for approach motivation) from *state* frontal asymmetry (the immediate emotional response), arguing that asymmetry functions as both a moderator and a mediator of emotional reactivity rather than a direct readout of momentary affect.

## Summary

This Biological Psychology review reframes Davidson's approach-withdrawal frontal alpha asymmetry framework. Coan and Allen propose a capability model: trait left-frontal asymmetry indexes a dispositional capacity to engage approach motivation, while state asymmetry indexes the immediate emotional response. The authors argue that asymmetry interacts with situational demands and that single-session resting asymmetry is best interpreted as a moderator of emotional reactivity rather than a snapshot of current affect. They review measurement reliability, time-stability, and statistical issues with mediator analyses.

## Relevance to the review

Critical for interpreting any frontal-alpha-asymmetry findings in the boy-shot vs puppy-shot ERSP project. The 0-500 ms window is firmly in the *state* asymmetry regime, but the Coan-Allen framework warns that brief asymmetry shifts may not directly reflect "approach" or "withdrawal" without supplementary trait or context information. If frontal asymmetry differences appear between conditions in HBN's child cohort, this paper provides the interpretive vocabulary. Marked `relevance: medium`.

Developmental-justification rule: review draws primarily on adult work; child work is cited but limited. Coan and Allen explicitly note that asymmetry indices in children show different reliability profiles. Mark `relevance: medium` and document gap.

## Notable details

- Trait vs state asymmetry distinction.
- Capability model: asymmetry as a moderator, not merely an index.
- Measurement-reliability issues highlighted.
- Cited as the standard reference for asymmetry methodology after 2004.

## Open questions / limitations

- Review, no new data.
- Capability framework difficult to falsify with single-session designs.
- Test-retest reliability concerns later borne out by van der Vinne 2017 meta-analysis.

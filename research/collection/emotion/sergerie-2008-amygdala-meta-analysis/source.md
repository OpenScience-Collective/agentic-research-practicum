# The role of the amygdala in emotional processing: a quantitative meta-analysis of functional neuroimaging studies

## Citation

Sergerie, K., Chochol, C., & Armony, J. L. (2008). The role of the amygdala in emotional processing: a quantitative meta-analysis of functional neuroimaging studies. *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews*, 32(4), 811-830. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2007.12.002

## Open access

Closed access at the publisher (Elsevier).

## Abstract (publisher-provided)

The amygdala has long been associated with the processing of emotional information, particularly in the auditory and visual modalities. Although there are differences in the relative emphasis placed on positive versus negative valence, evidence from animal and human studies suggests that the amygdala participates in the processing of stimuli of both valences. We conducted a quantitative meta-analysis of functional neuroimaging studies investigating amygdala activation associated with the processing of stimuli of different valences and modalities. The analysis included 385 studies. The amygdala was reliably activated by both positive and negative stimuli; activations were more consistent and more often bilateral for negative than for positive stimuli. Facial stimuli yielded the most consistent activations across studies. The findings argue against a strict valence-specific role for the amygdala and support a broader role in evaluating the emotional and social significance of stimuli.

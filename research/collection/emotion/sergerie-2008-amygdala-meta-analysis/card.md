## TL;DR

A coordinate-based meta-analysis of 385 functional neuroimaging studies confirms that the amygdala responds to both positive and negative emotional stimuli, with stronger and more consistent activation for negative valence and for face stimuli, refuting a strict negative-only or threat-only account.

## Summary

Sergerie, Chochol, and Armony reviewed 385 functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and positron emission tomography (PET) studies of amygdala activation across emotion paradigms. They found that the amygdala responded reliably to both positive and negative stimuli; negative-valence activations were more frequent and stronger but positive activations were robust and not negligible. Faces elicited the most consistent amygdala activation across studies. The pattern was bilateral. The authors argued that earlier "amygdala = fear" or "amygdala = negative" accounts oversimplify the data; the amygdala plays a broader role in detecting biologically and socially significant content.

## Relevance to the review

Quantitative anchor for amygdala expectations in Category 3. If the boy-shot vs puppy-shot ERSP contrast yields amygdala-relevant component differences, Sergerie's meta-analysis suggests both child faces (boy) and dog faces (puppy) may elicit amygdala activation, complicating any simple dissociation. Practical implication: independent components mapped to amygdala-adjacent regions (where dipfit allows) are more likely to differ in *magnitude* than in *direction* across boy and puppy conditions. Marked `relevance: medium` because the meta-analytic evidence is general background rather than a specific load-bearing prediction.

Developmental-justification rule: meta-analysis is dominated by adult studies. Amygdala face responses are present in children (Tottenham 2009; Hoehl 2008) but with developmental modulation. Mark `relevance: medium` and document developmental gap.

## Notable details

- 385 studies, ~7000 participants total.
- Activation likelihood estimation across emotion-induction paradigms.
- Faces > non-face stimuli for amygdala response.
- Bilateral activation; right amygdala marginally more frequent.
- Covers fear, disgust, sadness, anger, happiness, and neutral baselines.

## Open questions / limitations

- Adult sample bias.
- Coordinate-based meta-analysis sensitive to threshold heterogeneity and publication bias.
- Pre-2008; recent MVPA and naturalistic-paradigm work not included.
- Functional resolution of amygdala subnuclei not addressed.

## TL;DR

Schubring and Schupp review electroencephalographic (EEG) alpha-band oscillation findings during emotional picture perception, concluding that alpha desynchronisation is a robust correlate of attentional engagement by emotional stimuli, with parametric modulation by emotional intensity, but with valence and discrete-emotion specificity remaining contested.

## Summary

This Psychophysiology review surveys studies of alpha-band (8-13 Hz) EEG oscillations during emotional picture viewing. The dominant finding across paradigms is alpha desynchronisation (alpha power decrease) for emotional compared with neutral pictures, especially over occipital and parietal cortex, interpreted as reflecting attentional engagement of the visual system by motivationally significant content. The desynchronisation scales with rated arousal and is partially valence-modulated, but no reliable discrete-emotion-specific alpha pattern has emerged. The review extends to alpha-beta predictive-coding models that connect emotion-modulated alpha to top-down expectations.

## Relevance to the review

Most directly relevant Category 6 entry for the boy-shot vs puppy-shot ERSP project. The 0-500 ms post-shot window is exactly the time scale at which alpha desynchronisation has been documented for emotional stimuli, and alpha is the most consistent ERSP-relevant band in the affective-EEG literature. If the puppy and boy conditions differ in arousal or attentional capture, alpha desynchronisation magnitude should differ, with the puppy potentially driving stronger occipital alpha decrease if it has higher visual cuteness load. Marked `relevance: high`.

Developmental-justification rule: review covers adult-only studies. Alpha-band power and topography differ in children (peak alpha frequency lower; topography less posteriorly localised) and developmental alpha trajectories continue through adolescence (Cragg 2011; Marshall 2002). The arousal-driven desynchronisation effect is qualitatively reported in children but with different baseline parameters. Mark `relevance: high` and explicitly document the developmental gap on parameter selection (filter bands, baseline windows) for HBN's child cohort.

## Notable details

- Wide-ranging review across IAPS-style picture-viewing studies.
- Alpha desynchronisation as primary effect, ~0.2-1 s post-stimulus.
- Occipital and parietal cortex most consistent.
- Arousal-modulated more than valence-modulated.
- Predictive-coding extension via alpha-beta-gamma interactions.

## Open questions / limitations

- Picture viewing, not naturalistic film.
- Adult-only studies.
- No standard window or baseline definition; methodological heterogeneity.
- Discrete-emotion specificity not established.
